Guide to Reading Samuel Sullivan Cox's Manuscript Journal

The legibility of handwritten documents varies widely. An individual's handwriting may be inherently easy or difficult to read, but the legibility of that same person's handwriting will vary greatly depending on circumstances: infirmity caused by age or ill health, available tools (a dull pen, runny ink, rough paper), surroundings (trying to write in dim light or on a tossing ship), mood (writing in a hurry or under duress), accident (spilled coffee), deliberate alterations (crossing something out), the degradation of the written artifact (fading ink, crumbling paper), and so on. Moreover, the form of individual letters may vary within a document written by the same person according to the context in which each letter is written: for instance, the letters or punctuation next to a given instance of a letter. With all this potential variation in mind, the chart below can serve only as a general aid to reading. The following methods may provide additional help with deciphering words that are difficult to read:

  • Reading in context. Ask yourself what word is most likely to occupy the spot in the sentence where you are stuck. Look to the topic of the surrounding paragraph or the entire document as well.
  • Consulting A Buckeye Abroad. Because Cox expanded his journal into a published book, A Buckeye Abroad (1852), some transcription problems can be solved by consulting the published account of his travels. Especially helpful are the online, searchable editions of A Buckeye Abroad available through the University of Michigan's Making of America collection and the Internet Archive's American Libraries collection (click to access the Making of America edition or the American Libraries edition). Note: For the purposes of diplomatic transcription, keep in mind that even apparently parallel passages in the published book and the manuscript journal may differ.
  • Consulting a reference dictionary. The Oxford English Dictionary (OED) provides historical examples of usage and definitions for multiple senses of words, including senses no longer in common use, all of which can help with deciphering historical documents. In addition, online dictionaries such as the OED often provide search features that allow you to use them as "crossword-puzzle" dictionaries—you can replace letters you can't read with a "wild card" character and search for all words that match a pattern (e.g., a search for "f??d" will return "food," "feed," "fend," "fard," and 36 other words. Ohio State University users can log in to use the OED online.
  • Comparing letterforms. Often it is helpful to look at known words elsewhere in the document or the person's writing where the letters you think you might be looking at occur. In the table below, we list examples of the numerals 0-9, uppercase and lowercase letters A-Z, and some common marks of punctuation. Because the table provides the examples in word or phrase contexts, one can actually find multiple examples of most letterforms. In a few cases of critical, especially variable, or hard-to-read characters, we provide multiple rows with examples of a given letterform. If a letter doesn't appear in the table (e.g., capital X), no clear example of that letterform occurs in the journal.
  • Tracing letters. Sometimes, tracing the word you are trying to decipher, either by looking at the screen and writing on paper or by printing out the page and tracing over the word, can help to solve a transcription problem.
  • Having someone else read the letter aloud. Finally, it is often helpful to enlist another pair of eyes. While a partner reads a passage aloud slowly, identifying all capital letters and marks of punctuation, follow along in the manuscript.
