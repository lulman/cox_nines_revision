Introduction

Samuel Sullivan Cox (1824–1889) served as a Democratic congressman in the United States House of Representative from 1856 until his death, save for a few short interruptions, first representing his native Ohio (he was born in Zanesville) and then, from 1868 onward, his adopted state of New York. As a public servant, he is most remembered, even revered, for strengthening the Life-Saving Service (a precursor to the Coast Guard) and improving the working conditions of mail carriers. As a Democratic politician, his legacy is marred by his oft-assumed role as spokesperson for his party's resistance to abolition and emancipation during the years leading up to the Civil War. As an author, Cox published articles and books throughout his life on political and popular subjects, including six travel books. But in May 1851, Cox was a twenty-six-year-old Brown University graduate who had moved back to Ohio to practice law and had recently married (Peskin). On May 7, he and his wife Julia (née Buckingham, whom he married in October 1849), along with her brother Philo Buckingham and a cousin, Lucy Sturges, set sail on a long-delayed honeymoon tour of Europe and the Orient (Linsey 204–205).

The manuscript journal presented in this edition records Cox's experiences and reflections during the trip, but it also serves as a sketch pad for an expanded account of the voyage that Cox published the following year, A Buckeye abroad; or, Wanderings in Europe and in the Orient (1852). Appearing seventeen years before Mark Twain's more famous debut as a travel writer, The Innocents Abroad; or, The New Pilgrims Progress (1869), A Buckeye Abroad is still far from the first account in English of a "grand tour" of Europe. Both Twain and Cox offer similar apologia for publishing yet another account of a grand tour. Twain presents himself as everyman, rather than guide or critic:


Yet notwithstanding it is only a record of a picnic, it has a purpose, which is to suggest to the reader how he would be likely to see Europe and the East if he looked at them with his own eyes instead of the eyes of those who traveled in those countries before him. (xvii)

Cox presents a similarly self-effacing ethos:

The only merit reserved for a volume of travels is, either that the ground is untrodden, or that the mode of observation is new and peculiar. The author can lay no claim to the former. Something may be conceded to him, from the fact imported by the title—A Buckeye Abroad. A native of the west, and of that part, familiarly known as the Buckeye State,—may be supposed to look upon the scenes and mingle with the throngs of the Old World with new and peculiar sensations, which may find sympathy, if not with the general reader, at least with readers in Ohio. (5)

Of course, the degree to which Cox (or Twain, for that matter) presents "new and peculiar" views of Europe to his contemporaries is open to interpretation. In Cox's notes about each stage of his party's journey, however, twenty-first-century readers are sure to find insights into an historical and cultural milieu removed in time and sensibility from our own—even as we recognize long-enduring conventions of travel and tourism as well as familiar dynamics of cross-cultural encounters.


The Second Day Out (Babcock)

Crossing the Atlantic

Describing his party's departure from Jersey City, NJ in the RMS Asia on May 7, Cox conveys excitement, confidence, and eager anticipation. Still within sight of land, he enthuses, they spotted a young whale: "Hurrah! He spouted away throwing the spray 20 ft. high. No sensation of sickness yet. All is exhileration [sic]." Not for long. On May 8th, he writes with determination, "Sick to day, but able to be about. . . . I had to keep on deck today—or give in." The next day his mood turns: "Lay wretchedly all morning," he tells us, "Realized the horrors." Then he falls silent until May 13, unable in the interim "to do anything except lay sick." Cox's bout with sea-sickness was not the result of a particularly weak stomach; rather, it reminds us of the rigors of the early days of transatlantic passenger service on small steamships. The Asia, the largest ship in the British and North American Royal Mail Steam Packet Company's fleet when it was built the year before Cox's trip, was a wooden-hulled sidewheeler, 266 feet long by 40 wide with a gross tonnage of 2,226. By contrast, the current flagship of the Cunard Line, the Queen Mary 2, is 1,132 feet long by 135 feet wide abeam with an approximate gross tonnage of 151,400 (Cunard Line). The ride, in short, was rough in any seas. These early pages of Cox's journal also contain more subtle evidence of how violently the ship would pitch and roll. While most of the journal is written in ink, the pages composed at sea are written first in pencil, then traced over at some later date in ink. Presumably, Cox didn't trust himself on the bounding main with an open bottle of ink and a fountain pen!

In light of the discomfort he describes in ever more vivid prose (at one point he laments that his sensibilities have been reduced to the "nauseate sublime"), there is more than a little dramatic irony in his fascination with the technology of sailing. "Was there ever such perfection of enginery," he gushes at one point, "so massive yet so perfect; so powerful so fit." Yet he captures the public's fascination with the rapidly advancing technology of steamship travel, particularly the competition between the British Cunard Line and the American Collins Line during the 1850s to build ever bigger, more powerful ships that would provide the fastest, most elegant transatlantic crossing (Babcock 91–101). Cox provides a front-row view of that international competition in his description of the Asia's encounter with the U.S. steamer Humboldt (not a Collins ship) and his speculation that the Asia will beat the best crossing time of the Collins steamer Pacific.

Cox's prose, as much as his subjects, characterize him as a writer developing his distinctive voice. His writing—and sensibilities—are clearly informed by his reading. In the space of a single journal entry on May 13th, he quotes Washington Irving, Robert Burton, William Wordsworth, Jonathan Sewall, and Samuel Taylor Coleridge. He also draws on a rich—if sometimes overwrought—pictorial imagination, an ability to sketch sentimental and often didactic tableaux in words. Describing a small bird he sees somewhere off the coast of Nova Scotia or Newfoundland, Cox writes,


How beautiful would be the song of that little chorister upon a "bough swaying in the breeze" in some sequestered nook amid a leafy valley surrounded with green prospects & smiling cultivation. How like a hymn to its Creator would go up its carol to the All Audient creator;—yet here it is, with fagged wing & panting breast, contending with harsh cold blasts, just able to overtop the snow spray of the mid Ocean,—deluded from its home in the bosom of the wood by the mysteries of the Unknown, a thing of song in a sea of chaos—soon to [be] whelmed forever! Is it not an epitome of Man when he breaks the golden chords of that harmony which binds him to his God!

Cox's skill at this sort of writing would earn him the sobriquet "Sunset Cox" after the appearance in 1853 of an effusive description of a sunset published in The Ohio Statesman, a political journal that he owned and edited in Columbus, Ohio. Introducing a reprint of Cox's sunset essay in a 1910 volume of Ohio History, E. O. Randall calls Cox a "master word painter" (331). However, in his Preface to A Buckeye Abroad, Cox somewhat more modestly characterizes his literary allusions and descriptive prose as spontaneous plein air daguerreotypes of the scenes before him:

The impressions herein recorded were mostly taken upon the spot, and the allusions, historical, classical, or otherwise, were not sought for, but sprung out of the time and locality. Each lineament of each form in Nature or Art, each custom and characteristic were daguerreotyped, though somewhat rapidly, if not imperfectly, from the original, as it appeared in itself and in its environment. (6)

While it lies beyond the scope of this introduction to test Cox's claims, it is worth noting here that the journal, from the early entries onward, provides a rough means of gauging the spontaneity and directness of the writing in A Buckeye Abroad and the degree to which it results from revision and expansion.

As the Asia approaches Great Britain, Cox regains his sea legs and his appetite, finding the energy to explore the ship and even to appreciate, once again, the grandeur of the open ocean. Nevertheless, upon sight of the English coast, he enthuses, "And this is England! Glorious old England."

Liverpool

Cox's entries from Liverpool convey contagious amazement: "How can I describe my feelings at the grand illumination?" Inevitably, his impressions are filtered through his reading and cultural biases: He first introduces us to the social scene of Liverpool by describing a street urchin as a "veritable O. Twist, who in his tatters and with a bow & a touch of an apochryphal [sic] cap requested to carry the carpet bag."

With a storyteller's ear for dialogue, he transcribes the speech of colorful characters: "Soon came upon another liar. 'Waterloo Sir?' Sorry it's full Sir, I am boots there' Sir three ships just in hrs—Victoria Hotel is near—great hotel." With a travel writer's eye for landscape, architecture, and monuments, he provides a walking tour of Liverpool and its environs: "The Corinthian elegance of the architecture here & the neatness & well pavedness of the streets are permanent." And with a sketchbook artist's attention to detail, he occasionally draws a sketch in the margins of the journal.

London

Arriving in London on 19 May, Cox spends his first full day, Tuesday, May 20th, visiting the famous Crystal Palace, which housed the Great Exhibition of 1851, to view its "splendid contents." Over the next three days, he and his companions take in the Crystal Palace, an opera, the Epsom Derby, and a host of other tourist sites. They even indulge in a bit of paparazzi-style rubbernecking at royalty, seizing a chance to chase down Queen Victoria while riding in a hansom cab: "I asked the driver if it was the Queen, he said Yes—'Drive after them!' Away went the sovereign of Gt B. Away after went us Sovereigns in chase. . . . we got pretty close. Whereupon to our open Eyes and outstretched necks, the Queen herself turned round & gave us a good look & full view."

In addition to taking in the cultural sights—galleries, exhibitions, monuments, horse races, and royalty—this newly minted lawyer and future career politician makes a point of visiting the British Parliament. On May 23, he describes the (to his sensibilities chaotic) scene at the House of Commons and pastes into his journal a clipping from the Times recounting a debate on the Ecclesiastical Titles Assumptions bill.

Cox occasionally leavens his veneration of all things British with a pinch of Yankee pride, as he does when he declares the Princess Theatre at best only on par with the grandest theaters in the United States.

The journal also reveals the quotidian trials and details of extended international travel in 1851 (most of which still ring true today): Cox more than once loses track of the day and date, he repeatedly visits banks to deal with business back home or manage his funds (he stops by Barings bank in London), and he must regularly secure visas for upcoming legs of the journey.

Paris and Southern France

Cox arrives in Paris interested mainly in obtaining passports before moving on to Italy (or, more exactly, the various states that would comprise a united Italy after the 1860s and 70s), spending only one full day in the French capital, though a day he describes as "A perfect day of wonders." One special wonder turns out to be his own ability to speak French with his cabman: "My proficiency astonished me. Guess we can get along thro' France."

As the party heads south toward Italy and Rome, Cox takes particular note of the "Roman monuments in Gaul." Upon seeing a ruined viaduct in Avignon (probably the Pont du Gard) near which a proud new suspension bridge has been built, he exclaims, "How the old and new civilization is contrasted."

Sardinia and Tuscany

Traveling from Marseilles to Genoa (Sardinia), Cox encounters—and describes—other travelers from the U.S., as he does occasionally during his travels. On board the Languedoc, he meets the author Ann Sophia Stephens, who is traveling with George and Julia Pratt, the children of Judge Zadoch Pratt of New York. Cox describes Mrs. Stephens somewhat cryptically as "caustically agreeable."

Arriving in Leghorn (Tuscany) on June 4, Cox finds another reminder of home—the U.S.S. Mississippi—anchored in the port. On board the party meets the American sculptor Hiram Powers, most famous for his statue of the "Greek Slave" (1843), which was on display at the Great Exhibition. Significantly, given the political turmoil in Europe at this time, Cox's party will encounter several other U.S. warships during their travels, and Cox occasionally records discussions of politics with the locals.

"Eternal Rome" and the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies

Upon his arrival at the port of Civita Veccia, Cox offers his "verdict re. Italians," contrasting the "higher classes," whom he finds "decent, polite & refined," with the "lower classes," who "look hard, in dirty tatters, and with saucy deportment — mishaven [sic] and sallow—& seemingly incapable of a noble thought." He laments that they represent "the degenerate scion of the old Roman tree!" This tendency to interpret the appearance and living conditions of the poor in terms of character is all too familiar, but it is especially troubling and ironic given Cox's attention to political oppression elsewhere (later in his travels, he rails against Ferdinand II, "the brutal despot of Naples").

Rome, however, elicits Cox's veneration of Roman antiquities, which he describes with great detail in lengthy journal entries.

The "Dreamy Orient."

By contrast, Cox writes relatively little about Athens and Greece (and spends relatively little time there). En route to Constantinople via Smyrna, he describes islands nestled between the coasts of Greece and Turkey in the Aegean Sea: Tenedos, Lemnos, and Lesbos. After weaving through the Aegean, threading the Dardanelles, and steaming up the Bosphorus, the party finally arrives in the "Dreamy Orient."

Accompanied by an American consul and local firman, Cox and his company explore mosques (including the Hagia Sophia), seraglios, harems, splendid flower and fruit gardens, fountains of goldfish, and the "richly ornamented" tomb of Sultan Mahmoud II.

Stopping at Smyrna on their return, Cox and Philo take a traditional Turkish bath, and Cox records a sensuously detailed account of the process: "The attendant rubs us with glove hand with warm water, which is grateful & good. He pours bowl on bowl over the entranced body until the frame almost chills with delight. The head forms the point from which the dripping water falls all around. A soft & pleasant sensation comes over the mind; not intense & thrilling; but mellowed & tender as a sunset would produce or a picture of Murillo. Soon P & I began to throw the warm water at each other; in the mean time the sweat running, & the sensation robbing the body of all save enjoyment." Not surprisingly, Cox omits most of this private description from A Buckeye Abroad.

Trieste, Venetia, and Lombardy

Cox's party returns to Italy at Trieste (at the time, part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire), then heads west to Venice, Verona, and Brescia on the way to Milan and Lake Como, crossing Venetzia and Lombardy. In Trieste, we are reminded again of the tumultuous history of mid-century Europe: "Germans & Italians, Russian Soldiers & Austrian, peasants & merchants, all mingling in the shouts & Joy of a Sunday night." At Verona, Cox again encounters Europe's nobility: "The train of the Elector of Saxony & Queen started just before ours. He stopped at our Hotel, beat us at the Tower this morng, & is in many respects an energetic fellow." Throughout these pages, though, Cox focuses on the stuff of travel writing: the people's dress and occupations, the landscape, architectural details, associations from literature and art. The party arrives in Lake Como on July 18, 1851. Cox has filled his journal and, before beginning a new volume, he takes a moment to reflect on his travels: "How much of the round Earth has been seen! What has it bought us? Humility, Charity Kindness & Tenderness, No, these are plants, which grow in the shade of home, not the sun & air of the World. It is to be hoped that the gentler virtues & the primal duties will be better cultivated hereafter in our own homes." Unfortunately, we do not have the second journal, but we can follow the rest of the voyage in A Buckeye Abroad.

Project Description

This edition is the latest in a series of collaboratively produced electronic editions of previously unpublished nineteenth-century American manuscripts held in the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library at The Ohio State University. Previous editions include "My Dear Elizabeth," five letters written by Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to her sister Elizabeth Palmer Peabody between ca. 1837 and 1868 (currently being revised), and "Dear Wife," twenty-one letters written by riverboat captain William B. Anderson to his wife Louisa while he plied the Ohio river during the height of the American Civil War, 1862–1864. These documentary editions are intended to provide wide exposure and access to manuscripts that might otherwise be difficult for scholars to discover and consult, and to provide users with a variety of tools for studying those texts.

Each text was edited in connection with an undergraduate or graduate course offered by H. Lewis Ulman, Associate Professor of English at The Ohio State University. The courses aim to provide students with an opportunity to explore issues of textuality and mediation that arise when our cultural archives spread from page to screen and from library shelves to networked databases. Electronic textual editing is our vehicle for examining what happens—and envisioning what might happen—when artifacts in one medium are represented in another medium, especially with regard to the interpretive work of reading. Electronic textual editions also provide a contact zone that can help us reflect on what manuscript, print, and "born digital" artifacts can tell us about their relationships to one another and their unique properties.

While providing a reliable textual edition is a sine qua non, the Cox edition also explores ways of telling four stories inextricably woven into our work: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts, the history of the physical journal (including its recent restoration), the editorial process that gave rise to this particular interpretation of the text, and the mediation of our electronic delivery system.

About the Source Document

Title: "Journal of a Tour to Europe"
Extent: 164 pp.


Cox journal before restoration
SPEC.RARE.73

Samuel Sullivan Cox's "Journal of a Tour to Europe" is a leather-bound notebook approximately 6.5 in. (16.5 cm.) tall by 4 in. (10 cm.) wide. Its pages are unlined and unnumbered. A label in the upper left-hand corner of the paste-down endpaper inside the front cover indicates that the notebook was "Sold by / Wm. W. Rose / Stationer. / 19 Wall St. N.Y." The following text is scratched into the outside of the original front cover: "S. Cox / Zanesville / Ohio / (U.S.A.)"

When acquired by the Ohio State University Libraries in 2005, Cox's journal was in very delicate condition. Its leather binding was cracked and peeled, the spine was missing, the stitching was so loose that the text block was in danger of falling apart, and several leaves had become separated from their conjugates or completely loose. Before the pages of the journal were scanned for this edition, the journal was disbound and the gatherings repaired by Harry Campbell, Book and Paper Conservator with the Conservation Division of The Ohio State University Libraries. Once scanning was complete, the volume was resewn and rebound. The original boards, binding, and sewing supports were retained and are boxed with the restored journal.

The state of the original volume makes complete description conjectural, but the following comparison between the orginal and restored volumes will help users relate the page images contained in this edition to the two states of the journal.

Original. The front endpapers may have consisted of four leaves as follows: the paste-down endpaper, two conjugate leaves, and (conjecturally) a missing flyleaf or stub, all forming a separate section at the front. The text block proper was a straightforward 16º: [A-H]10; 80 leaves. The back endpapers may have consisted of the paste-down endpaper and (conjecturally) a missing flyleaf or stub.

Restored. The front endpapers consist of the original paste-down endpaper with the inside hinge area reinforced with sympathetic repair tissue. The text block is 16º: [A]12 (+A1.2) [B-H]10; 82 leaves. The back endpapers consist of the original paste-down endpaper with the inside hinge area reinforced by sympathetic repair tissue.

The journal is written mostly in brown ink on off-white paper that has taken on a bluish tint over time. When at sea, Cox often wrote first in pencil, then at some later time traced over his text in ink, perhaps to avoid spilling or smearing ink when the seas were rough. In most places where this overwriting occurs, it closely follows and all but obscures the pencilled text. Where the texts diverge, the differences typically consist of changes to line breaks or punctuation that do not alter meaning. Nevertheless, in order to reflect this unique palimpsest-like character of the text, we encode passages consisting of both pencil and ink versions in which differences are legible. For those cases, a button in the right margin allows readers to toggle between the two versions.

The Samuel Sullivan Cox journal is the property of the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library of The Ohio State University Libraries. Any use of the material should acknowledge such ownership.

Columbus, OH, 2007-03-15.

Editorial Method

Scanning. The manuscript pages were scanned as 24-bit TIFF images at 400 dots per inch (dpi), then coverted to 8-bit JPEG images at 400 dpi and full quality before being loaded into the Media Manager application. All edges were overscanned by at least 1/4 inch. No color correction was used at any stage of image preparation, but a scan of a photographer's 18% grey card on the same scanner produced RGB values at the center of 91/95/107 after applying a 150% Gaussian Blur in Adobe Photoshop to average color values. Thus, a properly configured monitor should provide a reasonable approximation of colors on the manuscript pages. The pages were scanned while the original journal was disbound for restoration. As a result, the page images contain penciled page numbers in the lower left- or right-hand corners. The numbers were erased after the volume was reassembled and rebound.

Markup and Annotation. This transcription of Cox's journal is marked up according to a set of project guidelines derived from the Text Encoding Initiative's P4 version of its Guidelines. See below for an overview of the project's markup conventions and a link to detailed project guidelines. Annotations are limited to the following categories: 1) comments about the text that supplement the TEI markup; 2) biographical notes about Cox's party and acquaintances (we do not annotate persons easily identified using standard biographical dictionaries); 3) references to literary texts; 4) ships mentioned in the text; 5) references to further information about journal entries available in A Buckeye Abroad.

Proofing. After all markup of the text was completed, a team member who was not involved in the original markup read the text aloud to the principal editor, who checked the marked-up transcript. In addition, the HTML pages produced by XSL transformations were proofed for anomalies introduced by the transformations.

Production Location of Digital Assets. The TEI XML-compliant transcription of the Cox journal, along with four style sheets used to provide variant "views" of the edition, are stored on a server hosted by College of Arts and Sciences at The Ohio State University. High resolution scans of the manuscript pages, scans of plates from contemporary sources including A Buckeye Abroad, and photographs documenting the restoration of the Cox journal are stored in the Media Manager application hosted by the Colleges of Arts and Science at The Ohio State University. Video files documenting the restoration of the journal are stored on a QuickTime streaming server hosted by the College of Arts and Sciences, and the files constituting the project's Web site at http://people.cohums.ohio-state.edu/ulman1/SSCoxJournal/ are stored on a Web server hosted by the College.

Archiving of Digital Assets. All digital assets related to this edition are archived in The Ohio State University Knowledge Bank.

Encoding Conventions

Markup of Physical Structure and Page Layout. The manuscript pages, encoded by the empty page break or <pb/> element, are the primary physical structures represented in the markup. Each <pb> element includes an "mmid" attribute identifying the unique ID of a high-resolution scan of that image in the Media Manager application. Line breaks (i.e., lineation determined or influenced by the physical constraints of the page rather than compositional choices by the author) are encoded with the empty <lb/> element. No attributes are included in the <lb> element.

Markup of Textual Structure. The journal entry, encoded by a <div1> element with the attribute-value pair "type='Entry'", constitutes the primary textual structure represented in the markup. Each entry contains an <opener> element—which in turn contains a <dateline> element—and one or more paragraphs. Each dateline may include a <name> element with (optionally) a "type" attribute containing one of the following values: city, region; and a <date> element with a "value" attribute whose value is in the form YYYY-MM-DD.

Entries consist of a series of paragraphs. Where indicated by indentation of a line from the left margin of a page, line breaks part-way across a page, or additional white space between lines, paragraphs are encoded with the <p> element and no attributes.

If present, any prefatory text preceding the dateline may be encoded as a <head> element at the beginning of the entry (i.e., within the <div>element and before the <opener>).

Level of Transcription/Encoding. This markup scheme supports a modest range of presentations, from semi-modernized/corrected to semi-diplomatic. The basic transcription follows the manuscript verbatim. Various manuscript features (e.g., cancellations, interlinear or marginal additions, tears in pages) are encoded, as are regularized versions of proper names and dates and standard versions of nonstandard spellings and expansions of abbreviations.

Extensions to the TEI DTD. While the markup for this edition follows, for the most part, the TEI P4 DTD, various extensions listed in the DOCTYPE declaration at the beginning of the core TEI XML-compliant transcript of Cox's journal (SSCoxJournal.xml) facilitate inclusion of external digital files. The "mmid" attribute added to the <pb> element identifies the unique item identifier of images stored in the Media Manager application hosted by the Colleges of Arts and Sciences at The Ohio State University. The project's Web site contains complete markup guidelines.
